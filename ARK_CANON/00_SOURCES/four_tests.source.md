THE FOUR TESTS
The build & decision filter — companion to the Ark Constitution · Camp DaddyMan · D.A.D.D.Y. Inc.
Every meaningful product, feature, song, film, program, or policy decision should pass these — in order. The tests evolved as the thinking deepened; each one corrects the flaw in the one before it. Apply them as a cascade: a decision must clear all four, and the deepest test sits beneath them all.
1 · The Butterfly Test
Ask: Does this help the butterfly fly?
Status — flawed; kept only to show the progression.
A butterfly can fly all day and accomplish nothing. Flight is merely the ability, not the purpose. A feature that makes a user more capable but points that capability nowhere has passed a hollow test.
2 · The Egg Test
Ask: Does this help the butterfly pollinate and lay eggs?
Status — closer, but only half the ecosystem.
Purpose is what the ability is for — contribution, reproduction, continuation. But eggs hatch into a field. A healthy butterfly released into a poisoned field just produces more poison. Transforming one individual is not enough if the surrounding conditions keep producing the same harm.
3 · The Field Test
Ask: Does this improve the field? (not: does this improve the butterfly?)
Status — the working test.
Healthy butterflies cannot survive forever in a poisoned field, but a healthy field produces butterflies for generations. One person escaping while the field stays poisoned solves nothing.
Hidden cost — build for it: the field does not pay. The escaped-butterfly story is legible, photographable, and sellable; healing the field is slow, unseen, and produces no individual hero. The market will pull every quarter toward butterfly-counting. This is the structural reason the nonprofit (D.A.D.D.Y. Inc.) and the studio (DaddyMan Studios) must stay separate — field work lives where it is allowed to be slow and unprofitable; butterfly stories live where they fund it.
4 · The Father Test
Ask: Does this help someone become capable of standing without us? (not: does this make them more dependent on us?)
Status — the audit — it tests the other three.
The first three measure what the system produces. This one measures whether the system is willing to lose what it produces.
Dependency can look identical to success: memberships, subscriptions, attendance, followers, and views can all grow while the people inside become less capable. The Father Test names the disease directly — the need to remain needed — instead of chasing its masks one at a time.
It is the only test whose passing feels like loss: the capable one leaves. A father who has done his job well will often feel, in the moment, like one who has been abandoned. The companion truth: the one who is left well is not abandoned — he is vindicated. You tell the empty nest from the betrayal only by what continues. The grief is the price; the continuity is the receipt.
Operational measures
▪	Not “how many stayed?” but “how many became capable?”
▪	Not “how many depend on us?” but “how many now help others?”
▪	Choose maturity even when it costs retention.
The Test Beneath All Tests
Ask: Does this create more life than it consumes?
Not a business metric — a law of nature. Every healthy ecosystem passes it; every dying one fails it.
How to Use This in the Project
▪	Run any new feature, screen, tier, badge, notification, or copy decision through tests 3 and 4 explicitly before building. (Tests 1 and 2 are usually already passed by anything worth proposing.)
▪	If a feature increases engagement but reduces capability, it fails the Father Test — redesign or cut it.
▪	Never make XP / points / streaks the primary measurement. Measure Good Done (witnessed acts of Good, Creation, Preservation, Reconciliation), never attendance or spending.
▪	When in doubt, ask the deepest test: more life than it consumes?
